What Does a Sync License Cost?

There is no fixed price for a sync license. Fees range from a few hundred dollars to over a million — depending on the artist, the song, the territory, the duration, and how the music is used. This guide explains how sync licensing pricing works and why it matters for copyright disputes.

Why There Is No Standard Rate Card

Unlike mechanical royalties or performance royalties, sync licensing fees are negotiated per deal. There is no statutory rate. No central database of prices. No industry-wide schedule that dictates what a particular song costs to license.

This is by design. The value of synchronizing a specific recording with visual media depends on variables that change with every deal: who the artist is, how popular the track is, where the content will air, how long the license runs, and what type of media it appears in. A 15-second Instagram story for a local boutique is a fundamentally different transaction than a 60-second global television campaign for an automotive brand — even if both use the same song.

This lack of standardized pricing is precisely what creates disputes. When music is used without a license, the question becomes: what would this have cost if the user had obtained permission? Answering that question requires methodology, not guesswork.

Master vs. Publishing: Two Licenses, Two Fees

Every sync license involves two separate rights — and two separate negotiations.

Master Recording
The specific recording of the song. Typically owned or controlled by the record label or, in the case of independent artists, the artist themselves. The master right covers the actual audio you hear.
Publishing / Composition
The underlying musical work — the melody, lyrics, and arrangement. Controlled by the publisher or songwriter. This right exists independently of any particular recording of the song.

Both sides must be cleared separately, and each charges its own fee. In the industry, pricing is typically quoted “per side” — meaning a sync license cost is the sum of both the master fee and the publishing fee. When a sync valuation report shows $5,000 per side, the total license cost would be $10,000.

In practice, most sync deals operate on a Most Favoured Nations (MFN) basis. This means the master and publishing fees must match: if one side quotes a higher fee, the other side is entitled to the same amount. MFN is standard practice in the sync industry and ensures parity between the two rights holders. For valuation purposes, MFN means that the per-side fee effectively determines the total — multiply by two.

Six Factors That Drive Sync Pricing

Every sync deal — and every sync valuation — is shaped by the same fundamental pricing factors. Understanding these is critical for anyone assessing what a license should have cost.

1. Artist Profile
A globally recognized artist commands significantly higher fees than an independent or emerging act. Cultural footprint, chart history, social following, and perceived brand value all factor in. This is what our xSV™ framework captures as the Artist Sync Score.
2. Song Performance
Not every song by a major artist carries the same value. A track with 500 million streams, editorial playlist features, and chart presence will command a premium over a deep album cut. Our Song Sync Score quantifies this independently from the artist.
3. Territory
Licenses are priced per territory. A single-country license costs a fraction of a global (worldwide) license. The US, UK, and Germany are typically the highest-value territories for sync. Multi-territory deals multiply the base price.
4. Duration
How long will the music be used? A 3-month social media campaign costs less than a 12-month broadcast license. Perpetual licenses — increasingly common in digital — carry a significant premium. Some deals include renewal options that affect pricing.
5. Media Type
Where the music appears matters enormously. A TikTok sound costs less than a broadcast commercial, which costs less than a theatrical film placement. Paid media (advertising) typically commands higher fees than organic content or editorial use.
6. Exclusivity & Context
Exclusive licenses — where no other brand can use the same track during the license period — command a premium. Context also matters: a song used as a brand anthem carries more value than background music in a corporate video.

How Territory Affects Pricing

Sync licenses are priced by territory. A license for the United States costs more than a license for a single European market, and a worldwide license costs significantly more than any single territory. The price scaling is not linear — adding territories multiplies the base price, but a global license typically includes a discount compared to licensing every territory individually.

The Social Media Enforcement Gap

The fastest-growing area of unlicensed sync usage is social media. Brands, agencies, and content creators routinely use copyrighted music in commercial social media content without obtaining sync licenses. This is not a gray area — commercial use of copyrighted music in social media advertising requires a sync license, regardless of the platform’s blanket licensing agreements with labels and publishers.

Platform licenses (like those between Meta and major labels) cover user-generated content, not commercial advertising. A paid Instagram ad or a branded TikTok campaign featuring a copyrighted track without a direct sync license is an infringement — full stop.

The scale of this problem is massive. The enforcement gap in unlicensed commercial music use on social media is estimated at $37.5 billion. Most rights holders are not yet actively pursuing these cases, but the legal frameworks are in place and the tools to detect infringement are improving rapidly.

For rights holders considering enforcement, the first question is always the same: what is the claim worth? That starts with understanding what the sync license should have cost.
